## Seasonal Car Maintenance for Coal Region Drivers

Schuylkill County roads are tough on cars. Between the road salt, the potholes, the mountain grades, and the temperature swings from January's single digits to August's 90-degree heat, your car takes a beating that drivers in other parts of the state don't deal with. Here's how to keep your car running through every season.

### Winter: The Coal Region's Toughest Season

Winter in the Coal Region means salt, ice, and temperatures that test every system in your car. Here's your winter prep checklist:

**Tires**
- Switch to winter tires or at minimum, check that your all-season tires have at least 4/32" of tread. The penny test works: stick a penny in the tread with Lincoln's head down. If you can see the top of his head, you need new tires.
- Check tire pressure monthly. Cold weather drops tire pressure about 1 PSI for every 10-degree drop in temperature. Low pressure means less grip.
- If you drive mountain roads regularly (Routes 209, 125, or 901), winter tires are worth the investment.

**Battery**
- Cold weather is the number one battery killer. If your battery is more than 3 years old, have it tested before the first freeze. Most auto parts stores will test it for free.
- Clean any corrosion from battery terminals with a wire brush.

**Coolant/Antifreeze**
- Your coolant should be a 50/50 mix of antifreeze and water. This protects down to about -34°F — well below anything Schuylkill County throws at you, but a weak mix can freeze and crack your engine block.
- If you can't remember the last time it was flushed, have your mechanic check it.

**Windshield and Wipers**
- Replace your wiper blades. The ones that survived summer are probably leaving streaks by November.
- Fill your washer fluid with winter-rated fluid (rated to -20°F or lower). You'll go through a lot of it with all the road salt.
- Fix any windshield chips before winter. Temperature changes cause small chips to spread into full cracks.

**Emergency Kit**
- Keep in your car: blanket, flashlight, jumper cables, ice scraper, small shovel, cat litter or sand (for traction), phone charger, and bottled water.
- If you regularly drive rural routes, add flares or reflective triangles.

### Spring: Undo the Winter Damage

Spring is recovery season for your car. Here's what to focus on:

**Undercarriage Wash**
- Road salt is corrosive. Get a thorough undercarriage wash as soon as temperatures stay above freezing. Hit the wheel wells, brake components, and exhaust system.
- If you notice any rust bubbles forming, address them now before they spread.

**Alignment Check**
- Potholes and frost heaves wreak havoc on your alignment. If your car pulls to one side or your steering wheel isn't centered, get an alignment done.
- This is also a good time to inspect your suspension components. Listen for clunks or rattles over bumps.

**Brakes**
- Salt and moisture accelerate brake wear. Have your brake pads, rotors, and lines inspected.
- Squealing or grinding noises mean you've waited too long — get them checked immediately.

**Air Filter**
- Spring pollen season in the Coal Region is aggressive. Replace your engine air filter and cabin air filter.
- A clogged engine air filter hurts gas mileage. A clogged cabin filter means you're breathing all that pollen.

**Tire Swap**
- If you ran winter tires, swap back to your all-seasons once overnight temperatures consistently stay above 40°F. Winter tires wear faster in warm weather and have worse handling.

### Summer: Beat the Heat

Schuylkill County summers bring heat and humidity that stress your cooling system and AC.

**Air Conditioning**
- If your AC isn't blowing cold, don't wait until the July heat wave. Have it checked in late spring.
- Low refrigerant usually means a leak somewhere in the system. A proper diagnosis and repair is better than just topping off the Freon.

**Cooling System**
- Check your coolant level and condition. If it's brown or has particles in it, flush the system.
- Inspect hoses and belts for cracks, bulges, or wear. A burst radiator hose on Route 61 in August is nobody's idea of a good time.
- Make sure your radiator fan is working. Overheating in stop-and-go traffic is a common summer problem.

**Tires (Again)**
- Hot pavement accelerates tire wear and increases the risk of blowouts, especially on underinflated tires.
- Check pressure when tires are cold (before driving). The number on your door jamb sticker is the target — not the number on the tire sidewall.

**Oil**
- If you're due for an oil change, don't push it into summer. Heat breaks down oil faster. Stick to your maintenance schedule.

### Fall: Prep for What's Coming

Fall is your last chance to get ready before winter hits.

**Tires**
- Inspect tread depth. If you're borderline now, you'll be in trouble by January. Replace before the rush.
- If you use winter tires, schedule the swap for late October or early November. Tire shops get busy once the first snow forecast hits.

**Heater and Defroster**
- Test your heater and defroster before you need them. If the heater blows cold or the defroster can't clear your windshield, you have a problem that needs to be fixed before winter.
- A heater that doesn't work could indicate a failing thermostat, low coolant, or a clogged heater core.

**Lights**
- Days get shorter fast. Check all your lights — headlights, brake lights, turn signals, and reverse lights. Replace any burned-out bulbs.
- If your headlights are cloudy or yellowed, a headlight restoration kit or professional polishing makes a significant difference in visibility.

**Fluid Top-Off**
- Check and top off all fluids: oil, coolant, brake fluid, power steering fluid, transmission fluid, and washer fluid.
- This is a good time for a general inspection if you haven't had one recently.

### Year-Round Basics

No matter the season:

- **Follow your owner's manual maintenance schedule.** It exists for a reason.
- **Don't ignore warning lights.** A check engine light might be a loose gas cap or it might be a serious problem. Get it diagnosed.
- **Keep up with oil changes.** Every 3,000-5,000 miles for conventional oil, 5,000-7,500 for synthetic, or per your manual's recommendation.
- **PA state inspection is annual.** Don't wait until it expires. Schedule it early to avoid the rush and give yourself time to address any issues.

Coal Region roads are hard on cars, but regular maintenance makes the difference between a car that lasts 200,000 miles and one that leaves you stranded on the shoulder. Find a [trusted mechanic](/auto-repair/) and stay ahead of the maintenance schedule.
